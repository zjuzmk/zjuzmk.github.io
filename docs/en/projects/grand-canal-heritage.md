# Conservation of the Grand Canal in Urban China

**2015–2016** · MA Thesis · The University of Hong Kong

## Overview

This project, developed from my MA thesis at HKU, examines the mismatch between official heritage frameworks and local realities in the conservation of China's Grand Canal — a UNESCO World Heritage site stretching over 1,700 km. Focusing on the Hangzhou section, the study reveals tensions between the "Authorized Heritage Discourse" and the lived experience of urban communities along the canal.

## Research Questions

- How does the authorized heritage discourse shape canal conservation policy and practice in urban China?
- What happens when top-down heritage frameworks meet local urban development pressures?
- Whose heritage is being conserved — and for whom?

## Key Findings

The Hangzhou case reveals a fundamental mismatch: official heritage discourse prioritizes the canal's material authenticity and historical monumentality, while local communities relate to the canal as a living infrastructure embedded in everyday urban life. This disconnect produces conservation outcomes that satisfy global heritage standards but alienate local populations.
