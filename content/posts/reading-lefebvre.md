> "The production of space is not the construction of a particular space, but the expansion of the spatial practice of a society." — Lefebvre, 1974

<!--more-->

I've been re-reading Lefebvre's *La production de l'espace* alongside my fieldwork in Chongli. It reads differently when you're sitting in a café that was a construction site six months ago.

## Conceived, Perceived, Lived

Lefebvre's triad—conceived space (representations of space), perceived space (spatial practice), and lived space (spaces of representation)—has become almost too familiar in urban studies. But in Chongli, the triad becomes concrete:

- **Conceived**: The master plan by a Beijing design institute. Ski slopes, gondola lines, "European-style" town center. Clean lines on paper.
- **Perceived**: The daily rhythms of construction workers, investors checking progress, local residents rerouting around road closures. The space as it is practiced.
- **Lived**: The villager who says her old courtyard "feels smaller" after the new road was built next to it. The migrant worker who sleeps in the conference center that hasn't opened yet. The space as it is felt, imagined, resisted.

## What Lefebvre Doesn't Help With

The Olympic temporality. Lefebvre assumes a relatively stable spatial practice—the daily reproduction of social relations in space. But what happens when an entire spatial structure is produced in three years, used for two weeks, and then left to settle? The "spatial practice" itself is under constant disruption.

I've started thinking about this as **event-space**: a spatial formation where the production of space is organized around a temporal deadline rather than a stable social practice. The Olympics don't just produce space—they produce it on a schedule, and the schedule distorts everything.

## A Fragile Connection

I'm not sure this holds together as a theoretical contribution yet. But the intuition is: in event-driven urbanization, Lefebvre's triad doesn't map cleanly. Conceived and perceived space collapse into each other (the plan *becomes* the practice almost overnight), while lived space struggles to catch up.

This needs more work.
