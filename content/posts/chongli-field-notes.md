> The resort was empty. Not closed—empty. Three months after the Olympics, the machines were still running but nobody came.

<!--more-->

It was −18°C when I arrived in Chongli. The Olympic venues were technically operational, but the village felt like a stage set after the crew left. Gondolas ran on schedule, carrying nobody. The hot spring hotel had a single guest—me.

## What the Off-Season Reveals

During the Games, everything was spectacle. In January, the scaffolding shows. The investor's brochures promised a "four-season mountain resort"—ski in winter, hike in summer, conferences in between. But the summer trails weren't built yet. The conference center was a concrete shell with a banner that read "Opening 2023." The banner was faded.

I talked to a resort manager who had been there since 2019. He said the post-Olympic visitor numbers were 40% of what the business plan projected. "The government invested, the developers built, and now we're waiting for a market that may not exist."

## The Village Behind the Village

Five kilometers from the resort, the actual village of Taizicheng was a different world. The old houses—stone and brick, courtyards, the kind of architecture that tourists supposedly come to see—were being "renovated" into uniform folk-style guesthouses. The renovation was funded by a state poverty alleviation program. The villagers I spoke with were ambivalent:

- The rent income was real, but the contracts were 20-year lock-ins
- The "traditional" facades were designed by an architecture firm from Shenzhen
- The actual traditional houses—the ones not on the main road—were being demolished

One woman told me: "They made our village look like the village they imagined we had."

## A Note on Method

This was my third visit to Chongli. I've found that returning to the same sites at different seasons—Olympic season, off-season, shoulder season—reveals layers that a single visit can't. The spectacle is the surface. The off-season is the structure.
