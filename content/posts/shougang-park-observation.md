> The blast furnaces are preserved, but they look like they were cleaned yesterday. Because they were.

<!--more-->

I visited Shougang Park on a weekday in May. The former Shougang Steel Works—once Beijing's largest industrial employer—was repurposed as an Olympic venue and "industrial heritage park" for Beijing 2022. The Big Air ramp sits on top of the old cooling towers.

## The Aesthetics of Clean Ruins

Industrial heritage is supposed to convey authenticity: rust, decay, the patina of time. Shougang's furnaces are repainted. The rust is managed. The industrial structures are lit at night with LED strips that cycle through colors—a kind of industrial Disneyland.

This is not preservation. This is **branding through industrial aesthetics**. The factory becomes a backdrop for selfies, not a site for understanding industrial history.

## Three Layers

Walking through the park, I noticed three distinct spatial layers:

1. **The Olympic layer**: The Big Air ramp, the athlete apartments (now a hotel), the media center. Bright, new, branded.
2. **The heritage layer**: The preserved blast furnaces, the museum displays, the interpretive signs. Educational, curated, safe.
3. **The worker layer**: Almost invisible. A small memorial wall lists the names of workers who died on the job. It's behind the gift shop. The workers' housing—simple brick buildings from the 1960s—was demolished for parking.

The three layers don't talk to each other. The Olympic spectacle and the heritage narrative coexist by avoiding the question of what happened to the people who actually worked here.

## A Question I Keep Returning To

Who is this park for? Not the former steelworkers (most were relocated to Hebei). Not the local residents (the surrounding neighborhood was gentrified). It's for visitors—the Olympic tourist, the weekend photographer, the corporate team-building group.

Industrial heritage, in this version, is a form of **accumulation by dispossession** that wears the mask of preservation. The industry is aestheticized, the workers are erased, and the land is financialized through tourism and real estate.
